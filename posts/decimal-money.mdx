JavaScript's `Number` is a 64-bit float. That's fine for sensors but
absolutely catastrophic for money. Here's why:

```js
0.1 + 0.2 // → 0.30000000000000004
```

Multiply that error across hundreds of transactions and your reconciliation
report is off by cents — sometimes dollars. Auditors do not enjoy that.

## The fix: `decimal.js`

```js
import Decimal from "decimal.js";

const total = new Decimal("0.1").plus("0.2");
total.toString(); // → "0.3"
```

`Decimal.js` does arbitrary-precision arithmetic. Slightly slower than
native floats, but math you can trust.

## When to reach for it

- Anywhere money is added, subtracted, or multiplied
- Crypto holdings (precision matters even more — 18 decimals on Ethereum)
- Tax calculations
- Anything where rounding errors compound

## When you don't need it

- Display only (just use `Intl.NumberFormat`)
- Quantities of physical objects (use integers)
- Performance-critical loops that don't touch currency
